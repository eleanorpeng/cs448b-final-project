Precursor of Emoji

ASCII Art & Emoticons :-)

Before emojis were born, people already started using text as means to express emotion. In 1982, computer scienstist Scott Fahlman proposed using :-) and :-( to distinguish jokes from serious posts on Carnegie Mellon's BBS. In Japan, Kaomoji (^.^) were used to express emotion, which are read upright instead of sideways like Western emoticons. In early 1980s, Microsoft's Wingdings allowed users to send simple pictograms.

The Birth of Emoji

176 Pixels that Changed the World

In 1999, Shigetaka Kurita created the first set of 176 emojis for NTT DoCoMo in Japan. They were created to help him convey emotions through the limited number of characters in text messagess. The word "emoji" is actually Japanese: e (çµµ, 'picture') + moji (æ–‡å­—, 'character'). The resemblance to the English words were purely coincidental.

Global Expansion and Standardization

The Rise of Emoji

Apple included an emoji keyboard in the first iPhone for the Japanese market, which was quickly discovered by the users worldwide. In 2010, Unicode standardized emojis, allowing them to work across different devices. When Apple added the emoji keyboard to iOS in 2011, usage exploded globally, revealing a massive global demand.

Modern Era

Diversity & Inclusivity

Today, emojis reflect our diverse world. Skin tone modifiers were added in 2015, followed by gender-neutral options and culturally specific symbols. Emojis now evolve alongside society, capturing new norms and movements.
